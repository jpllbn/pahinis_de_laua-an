About Pahinis Festival

Discover the rich cultural heritage behind our sweet celebration

The Origins of Pahinis

The Pahinis Festival, derived from the local term "pahinis" meaning to produce or extract, was established to honor the traditional muscovado sugar production that has been the economic backbone of Laua-an, Antique for generations.

Started in the early 2000s, the festival has transformed from a small community gathering to one of Western Visayas' most anticipated cultural celebrations, drawing visitors from across the Philippines and beyond.

  • Last week of January each year

The Sweet Heritage of Muscovado

Harvesting

The process begins with the harvesting of mature sugarcane plants from the fields. Skilled workers cut the cane close to the ground to maximize yield.

Extraction

The harvested cane is crushed to extract the sweet juice, traditionally using carabao-powered mills called "trapiche" or modern mechanical presses.

Boiling

The extracted juice is boiled in large vats over an open fire. Impurities are skimmed off the surface throughout the process.

Reduction

The juice is continuously stirred and reduced until it becomes a thick, dark syrup, concentrating the sugars and developing the rich flavor.

Cooling

The thick syrup is transferred to cooling trays where it crystallizes as it cools, forming the traditional muscovado sugar.

Packaging

The cooled sugar is broken up, traditionally packed in banana leaves or modern food-grade packaging for sale and distribution.

During the Pahinis Festival, visitors can witness live demonstrations of this traditional muscovado-making process and even participate in hands-on workshops.

Cultural Significance

Community Pride

The festival strengthens community bonds as families work together in preparation and celebration, passing down traditions to younger generations.

Economic Significance

Muscovado production remains a vital source of livelihood for many families in Laua-an, with the festival bringing additional tourism revenue to the local economy.

Cultural Preservation

Through games, dances, and rituals, the festival preserves indigenous practices that might otherwise be lost to modernization, keeping ancient traditions alive.
